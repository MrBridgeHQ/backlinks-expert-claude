# Tactic Catalog - By Tier and Risk

This page is the playbook of *how to acquire links*, organized by tier. The doctrine (`link-building-doctrine.md`) explains what Google values; this page turns that into concrete tactics, each labeled with effort, risk, value type, and the property type it suits.

The organizing principle: **Tier-1 tactics earn editorial links that move rankings. Tier-2 tactics build foundational presence that delivers traffic and brand - not ranking credit - and carries risk if done at scale. The AVOID list manufactures fake endorsements that Google's spam systems exist to catch.** Spend most of your effort on tier-1, use tier-2 in moderation and honestly, and never touch the AVOID list for a real property.

Two posture reminders from the doctrine, applied throughout:

- **Money sites (revenue-bearing businesses) stay tier-1-heavy and conservative.** A wasted or risky link costs a real business real traffic. When in doubt on a money site, do less.
- **Product / tool pages on third-party platforms tolerate more tier-2.** There is little business reputation at stake on a marketplace listing or a tool landing page, so a broader foundational mix is defensible - but it is still mostly traffic/brand, not ranking, and "tolerate more" never means "spam".

Each tactic below carries a header line: **Effort · Risk · Value · Suits**. Risk is the *expected* cost given the realism principle - most low-value links are ignored, not penalized; "high risk" means a meaningful tail probability of an actual penalty or of net-negative profile signals.

## Tier-1 - Editorial / earned (the core; these move rankings)

These produce links a human editor chose to place on a relevant site. This is where the budget goes.

### Guest posts (on relevant, real sites)

**Effort: high · Risk: low (if editorial) → high (if optimized-anchor link-farm) · Value: ranking + traffic + brand · Suits: all properties**

You write a genuinely useful article for a relevant publication; the link to your property sits naturally in the body or author bio.

- **How:** Identify relevant sites that accept contributions, pitch a specific topic that serves *their* readers, write the piece (route prose through a prose-humanization pass), use a natural or branded anchor - never an exact-match commercial anchor.
- **The line:** The post must serve the host's audience, not merely rent the host's domain rating. Content placed only to borrow authority is site-reputation abuse (parasite SEO). Optimized-anchor guest-post links placed at scale are named link spam.
- **Money vs tool:** For a consumer/retail money site, target publications with editorial standards in that vertical (e.g. trade, lifestyle, events press). For product/tool pages, target dev, SEO, data, and automation publications. Tool pages can run a higher guest-post cadence; money sites should be selective and high-quality.

### Niche edits / link insertions

**Effort: medium · Risk: low (editorial, free) → high (paid-for-followed, optimized) · Value: ranking + traffic · Suits: all properties**

A link added into an *existing* relevant article on someone else's site, where your resource genuinely improves it.

- **How:** Find existing articles that discuss your topic and would be improved by linking your resource (a study, a tool, a definitive guide). Email the author with a specific, useful reason to add it.
- **The line:** Editorially justified, naturally anchored insertions are fine. **Paying a site to insert a *followed* link is a paid-link violation** unless qualified with `sponsored`/`nofollow` (at which point it passes no ranking credit). Watch for sites that sell insertions - that is the bought-followed-link trap.
- **Money vs tool:** Both, but the money-site version must be genuinely editorial. Treat any "we'll insert your link for $X dofollow" offer as AVOID.

### Digital PR / journalist outreach (HARO-style)

**Effort: high · Risk: very low · Value: ranking (high-authority) + brand · Suits: all, especially money sites**

You provide an expert quote, data, or commentary to a journalist; they cite you with a link from a news or authority site.

- **How:** Respond to journalist requests (HARO / Connectively-style services), pitch original data or a strong angle, be fast and quotable. Pair with a link-worthy asset on your own domain (a study, a statistic) that journalists want to cite.
- **Why it's top-tier:** These are the most defensible links possible - a real editor at a real publication chose to cite you. Zero policy risk when genuine.
- **Money vs tool:** Excellent for a money site (market data, pricing commentary, industry-trend angles) *and* for tool pages (technical/industry commentary, original benchmarks). The conservative money sites especially benefit because it is pure tier-1.

### Resource-page links

**Effort: medium · Risk: low · Value: ranking + traffic · Suits: all properties**

Many sites maintain curated "useful resources / tools / links" pages. You get listed when your resource genuinely belongs.

- **How:** Find relevant resource pages, confirm your property is a genuine fit, email the curator with a one-line reason it helps their audience.
- **The line:** Only pitch where you genuinely belong. A relevant resource-page link is editorial; spraying unrelated resource pages is low-quality directory behavior.
- **Money vs tool:** Tool pages fit dev/SEO/automation resource lists naturally; a niche money site fits its vertical's curated resource pages.

### Broken-link building

**Effort: medium-high · Risk: very low · Value: ranking + traffic · Suits: all properties**

You find a dead link on a relevant page, and offer your live resource as the replacement.

- **How:** Identify relevant pages with broken outbound links (to a topic your property covers), then email the owner flagging the dead link and offering yours as a fix. Highly white-hat because you're doing the host a favor.
- **Money vs tool:** Both. Strongest where you already have a substantive resource to offer as the replacement.

### Genuine forum / community participation

**Effort: medium (ongoing) · Risk: low if genuine, high if spammed · Value: traffic + brand (NOT ranking - links are usually nofollow/UGC) · Suits: tool pages primarily**

> This is the *honest* version of forum links. It belongs in tier-1 as a *participation* tactic even though the links themselves are tier-2 in ranking value, because done right it is relationship-building, not link-dropping.

- **How:** Earn trust first - post 5–10 genuinely helpful, zero-link replies before ever linking. Then link only when it directly answers the question, at most one link per reply. Read and follow forum rules (a ban deletes all your links). A cadence of roughly once a week is plenty.
- **Be honest about value:** Forum links are almost always nofollow/UGC. The value is **referral traffic, brand visibility, and topical presence** - not ranking credit. A nofollow link on a high-traffic, well-indexed thread sends real qualified visitors; a link on a dead forum sends nothing.
- **Money vs tool:** Best for product/tool pages (dev, SEO, hosting, data communities) where your audience actually lives. For a money site, use sparingly and only in genuine enthusiast/trade communities.

## Tier-2 - Foundational (risk-labeled; traffic/brand, mostly NOT ranking)

State this plainly to the user every time: **these are not ranking tactics in 2026.** Google's policy names most of them (forum-signature links, low-quality directory/bookmark links, comment links) as link spam, and the platforms apply nofollow/UGC. Their honest value is a baseline of brand presence, a little referral traffic, and minor profile diversity. Used in small numbers on the right properties they are fine; **done at scale they shift from "ignored" to "penalized" and they endanger money sites.** Use them as a thin foundation, never as a campaign.

### Profile links

**Effort: low · Risk: low at small scale, high if mass-created · Value: brand presence + occasional traffic; minimal-to-zero ranking · Suits: tool pages > money sites**

A link in your account/profile on a platform where you have a real presence (GitHub, dev communities, industry directories).

- **How:** Create profiles only where you genuinely participate or where the platform is itself relevant and authoritative (e.g., GitHub for a dev/tool audience). Fill them out as a real person/brand would.
- **The line:** A handful of profiles on relevant platforms = fine brand foundation. Mass profile creation across hundreds of unrelated "profile-creation sites" = scaled-abuse spam (the "9,000 profile sites" trap). Most profile links are nofollow.
- **Money vs tool:** Strongest for tool pages (GitHub, dev platforms). Money sites: only on reputable, relevant business/trade profiles.

### Directory submissions

**Effort: low-medium · Risk: low for niche/reputable, high for mass general directories · Value: referral traffic + local/niche discovery; minimal ranking · Suits: both, selectively**

Listing your property in a directory.

- **How:** Submit *only* to directories that are reputable, relevant to your niche or locale, and actually send traffic (industry directories, legitimate local business directories, curated tool catalogs).
- **The line:** Google names *low-quality* directory and bookmark links as link spam. The test is whether a directory has editorial standards and real users, or exists only to host links. Mass general-directory submission is wasted effort at best.
- **Money vs tool:** A money site: legitimate vertical/local business directories. Tool pages: curated tool/SaaS/MCP catalogs (note: a specific marketplace/directory channel is its own playbook - delegate to a directory/marketplace-distribution skill).

### Forum signatures

**Effort: low · Risk: medium-high · Value: minor traffic; effectively zero ranking · Suits: neither as a primary tactic**

A link in your forum signature that appears under every post.

- **How / honest take:** Google's policy *explicitly names* forum-signature links with optimized links as link spam, and forums nofollow them. A signature link is acceptable as a passive byproduct of genuine participation (tier-1 above), but creating posts *to surface a signature link* is exactly the spam pattern. Do not treat signatures as a tactic; treat them as a minor courtesy field on forums where you genuinely contribute.
- **Money vs tool:** Neither. Don't build campaigns on this.

### Blog comments

**Effort: low · Risk: medium-high · Value: occasional traffic; effectively zero ranking · Suits: neither as a primary tactic**

A link left in a blog comment.

- **How / honest take:** Comment links are nofollow/UGC and comment spam with optimized links is named link spam - hosts delete it anyway. A genuine, on-topic comment that happens to reference your resource on a relevant blog can send a trickle of traffic and build a relationship that *later* yields a tier-1 link. The link itself has no ranking value.
- **Money vs tool:** Use only as relationship-warming, never as a link tactic.

### Web 2.0 properties

**Effort: medium · Risk: medium (low if a genuine publication, high if a thin link-host) · Value: depends entirely on quality · Suits: tool pages, with caution**

Content you publish on a free hosted platform (Medium, dev.to, etc.) that links back to your property.

- **How:** Treat these as *genuine publishing channels*, not link silos. Publishing a real, useful article on dev.to that links your tool is legitimate content marketing (most such links are nofollow but drive traffic and audience). Spinning up thin "web 2.0" blogs solely to host followed links to yourself is the old PBN-lite pattern - net-negative.
- **The line:** Real audience + real content = fine (traffic/brand). Thin self-hosted link silo = AVOID.
- **Money vs tool:** Tool pages benefit from genuine dev-platform publishing. Money sites: prefer guest posts on established outlets instead.

## AVOID - Link schemes (penalized; never for a real property)

These manufacture fake endorsements. Each is a named or clear violation of Google's link-spam policy and carries genuine penalty risk. They are documented here so the agent recognizes and refuses them - not so they can be used.

### PBNs (Private Blog Networks)

Networks of sites you control (often on expired domains) that link to your money site. **Named expired-domain / link-spam abuse, high penalty risk.** Never for any real property. If a plan or a source proposes this, refuse and explain.

### Undisclosed paid *followed* links

Buying links that pass ranking credit. **Direct policy violation.** Paid links are acceptable *only* when qualified with `rel="sponsored"` or `rel="nofollow"` - at which point they pass no ranking credit and you're buying traffic, not rankings. Any "buy dofollow link / $X per followed link" offer (including the FR and EN link-selling marketplaces) is selling a violation. Disclose sponsorships; never buy followed credit.

### Mass automated submission

Bulk-submitting your property to hundreds/thousands of profile, directory, bookmark, or forum sites via tools or huge lists. **Scaled-abuse / machine-generated link spam.** Usually ignored (wasted effort), occasionally penalized, always low-value. The giant "1,352-site" / "9,000-profile" lists are sourcing pools to hand-pick a few relevant communities from - never to submit to en masse.

### Reciprocal-link schemes

Systematic "link to me and I'll link to you" arrangements. **Named link spam; discounted algorithmically.** An occasional natural mutual link between genuinely related sites is fine; a scheme is not.

### Exact-match anchor blasts

Pointing many inbound links at a page using the same exact-match commercial anchor. Creates an unnatural anchor distribution and triggers keyword-stuffing signals. Keep exact-match anchors rare (see `campaign-planning.md` for the mix).

### Comment / forum / profile spam at scale

The scaled version of the tier-2 tactics above. Crosses from "ignored" to "named link spam" and risks the host deleting your account (and links). The dividing line between tier-2 and this AVOID entry is *volume and genuineness*: a few genuine contributions vs. automated/templated mass dropping.

## How to use this catalog

1. **Start from the property's posture** (`property-profiles.md`): money site → tier-1-heavy, minimal tier-2; tool page → tier-1 core plus a broader-but-honest tier-2 foundation.
2. **Verify every target before acting** (`qualification.md`) - relevance, live dofollow status, authority band, spam signals, activity. Never trust a stale list's dofollow claim.
3. **Build the mix and pacing** in `campaign-planning.md` - tier ratios, anchor distribution, velocity, KPIs.
4. **Write the assets** with `asset-templates.md`, routing all prose through a prose-humanization pass so it reads human, and applying your tracking/referral param to every product/tool URL where one applies.
5. **Check it against the line** in `ethics-risk.md` before shipping - especially anything paid, reciprocal, or high-volume.

Programmatic discovery and DR/dofollow checks run via managed APIs (Firecrawl, etc.) or remote scraping infrastructure - **never local scraping**.
